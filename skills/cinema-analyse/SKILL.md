# Cinema Analyse

> Structural and sociological analysis of films. Goes beyond surface review to narrative architecture, thematic layers, political readings, and the human reality of production.

## When to Use

- Analyzing narrative structure of a film (three-act, non-linear, episodic)
- Thematic or political reading of a work
- Applying auteur theory to a filmmaker's body of work
- Understanding production context (budget, studio interference, labor conditions)
- Comparing analytical frameworks (formalist, semiotic, psychoanalytic, feminist, postcolonial)
- Writing or structuring a film analysis (memoire, critique, essai)

## How It Works

### 1. Separate description from interpretation

First establish what happens in the film (diegesis). Then analyze how it is told (narration) and what it means (interpretation). Never skip step one.

### 2. Choose an analytical framework

Different lenses reveal different things. Be explicit about which framework you are applying. A feminist reading and a formalist reading of the same film will produce different -- and potentially complementary -- insights.

### 3. Ground claims in evidence

Every interpretive claim must be tied to specific scenes, shots, sounds, or structural choices. "Kubrick is a perfectionist" is a cliche. "Kubrick's symmetrical compositions in *The Shining* create a sense of institutional control that mirrors the Overlook's domination of its inhabitants" is analysis.

## Narrative Structures

### Classical structures

- **Three-act structure**: Setup / Confrontation / Resolution. Syd Field's paradigm. Works for most Hollywood films. Inciting incident around minute 10-15, midpoint reversal, climax in act three.
- **Hero's journey** (Campbell/Vogler): Departure / Initiation / Return. Underlies *Star Wars*, most adventure and fantasy narratives. Useful but reductive if applied dogmatically.
- **Five-act structure**: Exposition / Rising action / Climax / Falling action / Denouement. Shakespeare's model, applicable to epic cinema.

### Non-classical structures

- **Non-linear**: *Pulp Fiction* (1994), *Memento* (2000), *Arrival* (2016). Temporal scrambling as thematic device -- memory, perception, trauma.
- **Episodic / mosaic**: *Amores Perros* (2000), *Magnolia* (1999), *Babel* (2006). Multiple storylines that intersect. Tests the viewer's capacity for pattern recognition.
- **Circular**: *Mulholland Drive* (2001), *Stalker* (1979). The ending returns to the beginning but the meaning has changed.
- **Minimalist / plotless**: Antonioni (*L'Avventura*, 1960), Tsai Ming-liang, Chantal Akerman (*Jeanne Dielman*, 1975). Narrative is deliberately withheld. The structure is durational, spatial, phenomenological.

## Analytical Frameworks

### Auteur theory

Originated in Cahiers du Cinema (Truffaut, "Une certaine tendance du cinema francais," 1954). The director as primary creative author. Look for:
- **Recurring themes**: Kubrick and control, Hitchcock and voyeurism, Varda and gleaning.
- **Visual signatures**: Wes Anderson's symmetry, Malick's magic hour, Villeneuve's scale.
- **Obsessions**: What does the filmmaker return to across their body of work?

Limits: auteur theory can erase collaborative contributions (cinematographers, editors, writers, actors). Always acknowledge the collective nature of filmmaking.

### Political reading

Every film is political, even those that claim neutrality. Neutrality is itself a political position. Analyze:
- **Who has agency?** Whose perspective structures the narrative? Who is absent?
- **What is normalized?** What social arrangements does the film present as natural or inevitable?
- **Historical context**: What was happening politically when the film was made? *Invasion of the Body Snatchers* (1956) cannot be separated from McCarthyism.
- **Production politics**: Who funded the film? Did censorship shape the text? Did state funding come with ideological strings?

### Feminist film theory

Laura Mulvey, "Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema" (1975). The male gaze: cinema structures looking so that the viewer identifies with a male subject and women are objects of spectacle. Essential framework, but also evolved significantly since 1975. Consider:
- Whose gaze structures the film?
- How are bodies filmed? For whose pleasure?
- Counter-examples: Sciamma (*Portrait of a Lady on Fire*), Campion (*The Piano*), Denis (*Beau Travail*).

### Postcolonial reading

- Whose stories are told? By whom? For whom?
- Representation vs. self-representation. A Hollywood film about Africa is not African cinema.
- Third Cinema's distinction: First Cinema (Hollywood), Second Cinema (European art film), Third Cinema (revolutionary, decolonial).
- The "festival film" trap: when films from the Global South are shaped by European festival expectations.

### Formalist / semiotic

Analyze the film as a system of signs. Shot composition, editing patterns, sound design, color palettes as a language. Metz's *Film Language* (1968). Useful for close reading, but risks ignoring social context if applied in isolation.

## Production Realities

### The human cost

Films are not just texts -- they are the result of labor. Analyze:
- **Working conditions**: Intermittents du spectacle (France), gig economy of film crews, 14-hour shoot days.
- **Power dynamics on set**: Director-actor relationships, Harvey Weinstein and #MeToo, the ethics of demanding "real" emotions.
- **Budget as constraint and enabler**: A $200M Marvel film and a $2M Dardenne brothers film are not just different scales -- they represent fundamentally different modes of production with different relationships to risk, creativity, and audience.

### Studio interference

- Orson Welles and *The Magnificent Ambersons* (1942) -- studio recut, original cut destroyed.
- Terry Gilliam and *Brazil* (1985) -- "Love Conquers All" vs. director's cut.
- The "director's cut" phenomenon: sometimes better, sometimes self-indulgent. Always politically significant.

### The economics of auteurism

Auteur cinema survives through: state funding (CNC in France, KOFIC in Korea), festival circuit, art house distribution, and increasingly, streaming platforms. Each funding source shapes what gets made.

## Examples

**Query**: "Analyse la structure narrative de *Memento*."

*Memento* (Nolan, 2000) uses two interleaved timelines: color sequences move backward, black-and-white sequences move forward. They converge at the film's end/story's middle. This is not a gimmick -- it structurally replicates the protagonist's anterograde amnesia. The viewer, like Leonard, cannot trust what came before. The unreliable narrator is built into the editing pattern, not just the dialogue. Compare with *Rashomon* (different perspectives on the same event) and *Eternal Sunshine* (emotional logic overriding chronology).

**Query**: "Kubrick est-il un auteur?"

Kubrick is the textbook auteur case: total control over every production aspect, recurring themes (dehumanization by systems, the failure of rationality, symmetry as oppression), visual signatures (one-point perspective, Steadicam corridors, cold color palettes). But the auteur lens risks erasing John Alcott's cinematography, Wendy Carlos's scores, and the actors' contributions. Kubrick's "authorship" was also enabled by unusual contractual power with Warner Bros. -- authorship is partly an institutional condition, not just a creative one.

**Query**: "Comment lire *Get Out* politiquement?"

*Get Out* (Peele, 2017) operates on multiple political registers simultaneously. Surface: liberal racism is more dangerous than overt racism because it is invisible. Structure: the horror genre's conventions (the vulnerable protagonist, the hostile space) map onto the Black American experience of navigating white spaces. The "sunken place" is a spatial metaphor for political silencing. The Coagula procedure literalizes the appropriation of Black bodies. Peele's choice of genre (horror, not drama) is itself political -- it refuses the "social issue film" respectability framework and instead weaponizes visceral audience response.
