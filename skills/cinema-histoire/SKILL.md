# Cinema Histoire

> Global cinema history from the Lumiere brothers to the present. Covers movements, key figures, and cultural contexts across all continents -- not limited to the Western canon.

## When to Use

- Questions about cinema movements and their historical context
- Tracing the evolution of styles, genres, or national cinemas
- Understanding who influenced whom across eras and borders
- Contextualizing a filmmaker or film within its historical moment
- Exploring cinema traditions outside the Euro-American mainstream

## How It Works

### 1. Situate the question historically

Identify the era, geography, and movement. Cinema history is not a single timeline -- it is a web of parallel, overlapping, and interacting traditions.

### 2. Connect context to creation

Films do not emerge in a vacuum. Political regimes, technological shifts, economic conditions, and cultural movements shape what gets made and how.

### 3. Trace influence chains

Show how movements respond to, react against, or build upon predecessors. The French New Wave cannot be understood without Italian Neorealism. Korean cinema's rise cannot be understood without the 1999 screen quota policy.

## Timeline of Major Movements

### Pre-cinema and Origins (1890s)

- **1895**: Lumiere brothers, *L'Arrivee d'un train en gare de La Ciotat*. Cinema as documentary impulse.
- **1896-1913**: Georges Melies. Cinema as spectacle, illusion, narrative (*Le Voyage dans la Lune*, 1902).
- **1910s**: D.W. Griffith develops continuity editing, cross-cutting, close-ups. *The Birth of a Nation* (1915) -- technically groundbreaking, ideologically reprehensible. This duality is essential to discuss honestly.

### Silent Era Movements (1919-1930)

- **German Expressionism** (1919-1927): *Das Cabinet des Dr. Caligari* (Wiene, 1920), *Nosferatu* (Murnau, 1922), *Metropolis* (Lang, 1927). Distorted sets, extreme shadows, psychological interiority. Fleeing artists later shaped Hollywood film noir.
- **Soviet Montage** (1924-1930): Eisenstein (*Battleship Potemkin*, 1925), Vertov (*Man with a Movie Camera*, 1929), Poudovkine. Cinema as political tool. Montage as the fundamental unit of meaning.
- **French Impressionism** (1918-1929): Abel Gance (*Napoleon*, 1927), Germaine Dulac, Jean Epstein. Subjective camera, superimposition, rhythm.
- **Surrealism**: Bunuel and Dali, *Un Chien Andalou* (1929). Cinema as dream logic.

### Classical Hollywood (1930-1960)

- **Studio system**: Vertically integrated majors (MGM, Warner, Paramount, Fox, RKO). Star contracts, genre conventions, Production Code censorship (Hays Code, 1934-1968).
- **Film noir** (1940s-1950s): Expressionist shadows + hardboiled narratives. *Double Indemnity* (Wilder, 1944), *The Third Man* (Reed, 1949). Often made by European emigres.
- **Orson Welles**: *Citizen Kane* (1941) synthesizes deep focus, non-linear structure, expressionist lighting. The film most cited as "greatest ever made" -- understand why, but also question the canon.

### Post-War Revolutions (1945-1970)

- **Italian Neorealism** (1945-1952): Rossellini (*Rome, Open City*, 1945), De Sica (*Bicycle Thieves*, 1948), Visconti. Non-professional actors, real locations, social reality. Direct response to fascism and war destruction.
- **Japanese Golden Age** (1950s): Kurosawa (*Rashomon*, 1950; *Seven Samurai*, 1954), Ozu (*Tokyo Story*, 1953), Mizoguchi (*Ugetsu*, 1953). Three radically different approaches to cinema from the same national tradition.
- **French New Wave / Nouvelle Vague** (1958-1967): Godard (*A bout de souffle*, 1960), Truffaut (*Les 400 coups*, 1959), Varda (*Cleo de 5 a 7*, 1962), Resnais (*Hiroshima mon amour*, 1959), Rivette, Rohmer, Chabrol. Cahiers du Cinema critics become filmmakers. Jump cuts, location shooting, direct address, auteur theory. EICAR's intellectual heritage.
- **Cinema Novo** (Brazil, 1960s): Glauber Rocha (*Black God, White Devil*, 1964). "An aesthetic of hunger." Third Cinema manifesto: cinema as revolutionary tool.

### Global New Waves (1960-1980)

- **New Hollywood** (1967-1980): Coppola, Scorsese, Altman, Spielberg, Lucas. Film school generation. *Bonnie and Clyde* (1967), *The Godfather* (1972), *Taxi Driver* (1976). Studio system collapses, auteurs rise, then blockbusters recapture the industry.
- **New German Cinema**: Fassbinder, Herzog, Wenders, von Trotta. *Aguirre, the Wrath of God* (1972), *Paris, Texas* (1984).
- **Third Cinema** (Latin America, Africa): Solanas and Getino's manifesto (1969). Cinema as decolonization. Ousmane Sembene (*Black Girl*, 1966) -- the father of African cinema.
- **Soviet poetic cinema**: Tarkovsky (*Stalker*, 1979; *Mirror*, 1975), Parajanov (*The Color of Pomegranates*, 1969).

### Modern Expansions (1980-2000)

- **Iranian New Wave**: Kiarostami (*Close-Up*, 1990; *Taste of Cherry*, 1997), Panahi (*The White Balloon*, 1995), Makhmalbaf. Cinema under censorship as creative constraint.
- **Hong Kong cinema**: John Woo (action ballet), Wong Kar-wai (*Chungking Express*, 1994; *In the Mood for Love*, 2000). Christopher Doyle's cinematography.
- **Dogme 95** (Denmark): Lars von Trier, Thomas Vinterberg. *Festen* (1998). Anti-spectacle manifesto. Handheld, natural light, no post-production music.
- **Nollywood emergence** (Nigeria, 1990s): Direct-to-video production model. Now the second-largest film industry by volume globally. Often ignored in Western film history courses.

### 21st Century (2000-present)

- **Korean cinema**: Bong Joon-ho (*Memories of Murder*, 2003; *Parasite*, 2019), Park Chan-wook (*Oldboy*, 2003), Lee Chang-dong (*Burning*, 2018). Korea's rise is partly enabled by state cultural policy (screen quotas, KOFIC funding).
- **Romanian New Wave**: Mungiu (*4 Months, 3 Weeks and 2 Days*, 2007), Puiu (*The Death of Mr. Lazarescu*, 2005). Minimalism, real time, social realism under post-communist conditions.
- **Digital revolution**: DSLR democratization (Canon 5D Mark II, 2008). Mumblecore. YouTube. Streaming platforms reshape distribution. *Tangerine* (2015) shot on iPhone.
- **French contemporary**: Sciamma (*Portrait of a Lady on Fire*, 2019), Audiard, Ducournau (*Titane*, 2021), Triet (*Anatomy of a Fall*, 2023), Lacuesta, Diop (*Atlantique*, 2019).
- **African contemporary**: Diop, Maiga, Ramata-Toulaye Sy. Growing international recognition beyond the Sembene legacy.

## Examples

**Query**: "Quelle est l'influence du neorealisme italien sur la Nouvelle Vague?"

Neorealism gave the New Wave its ethical and aesthetic foundation: shoot in real locations, use non-professional actors, reject studio artifice. Bazin championed both movements in Cahiers du Cinema. Truffaut and Godard explicitly cited Rossellini as a model. But the New Wave added self-reflexivity and formal experimentation that neorealism avoided.

**Query**: "Pourquoi le cinema coreen a-t-il explose dans les annees 2000?"

Three converging factors: (1) screen quota policy protecting domestic exhibition, (2) KOFIC state funding for production, (3) a generation of filmmakers trained during the democratic opening post-1987. Bong, Park, Lee, and Kim Jee-woon emerged from a culture that valued cinema as both art and popular entertainment, without the art/commerce divide that cripples many national cinemas.

**Query**: "Le cinema africain, ca commence ou?"

Ousmane Sembene, *Borom Sarret* (1963, short) and *Black Girl* (1966, feature). Senegalese. Former dockworker and novelist. Chose cinema because "in Africa, the cinema is the night school of the people." But African cinema is not a monolith: Nollywood (Nigeria) is mass entertainment, Fespaco (Burkina Faso) champions auteur cinema, and contemporary filmmakers like Mati Diop and Ramata-Toulaye Sy navigate between continents.
